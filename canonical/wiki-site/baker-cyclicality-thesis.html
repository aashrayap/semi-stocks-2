Baker's Cyclicality Thesis — Every Shortage Is Followed by a Glut

Source
tags[baker, cyclicality, thesis, shortage, glut, valuation, multiple-compression]
sources[sources/baker-q4-2025, concepts/bottleneck-cascade, concepts/memory-supercycle]
created2026-04-07
updated2026-04-07

Gavin Baker (@GavinSBaker, Atreides Management) articulated a framework for semiconductor investing in a November 24, 2023 tweet thread and expanded on it in his December 2025 “Invest Like the Best” podcast (EP.451). The core claims:

  1. Semiconductor companies trade like the cyclicals they have always been — even the most secular, highest-growth names experience massive inventory corrections every 2-3 years.
  2. Any semiconductor company whose products are in short supply will see significant multiple compression. Elevated lead times = lower multiples.
  3. The converse may also be true: stocks may hold up better or even rise when revenue declines 20-40% from peak.
  4. Every shortage is followed by a glut. (Paraphrasing Nassim Taleb: “I’ve seen gluts not followed by shortages, but I’ve never seen a shortage not followed by a glut.”)

Baker calls this the “iron law of economic history”: semiconductor inventory cycles are inevitable because customer buffer inventories must equal lead times. When lead times extend, customers double-order. When supply catches up, cancellations cascade.

This output evaluates Baker’s claims against 6 semiconductor subsectors from 2019-2026, using specific tickers, price data, P/E multiples, revenue figures, and lead time data.

Subsector Deep Dives

1. Memory — MU (The Canonical Cycle)

The DRAM/NAND cycle is Baker’s Exhibit A. Three complete cycles in 8 years:

Cycle 1 (2017-2020):

  • Shortage: DRAM prices tripled 2016-2018. MU revenue hit $30.4B (FY2018), EPS $11.51.
  • Glut: Revenue fell 30% to $21.4B by FY2020. DRAM prices fell 50-60%.
  • Trailing P/E at peak earnings: 2.96x — the lowest in the S&P 500. The market screamed “these earnings aren’t sustainable.” Stock fell 56% ($64 -> $28).

Cycle 2 (2020-2023):

  • Shortage: WFH + 5G + gaming demand. Revenue back to $30.8B (FY2022). Lead times stretched to 20-30 weeks.
  • Glut: Revenue halved to $15.5B (FY2023). Net income swung from +$8.7B to -$5.8B. Inventory hit 31 weeks of supply.
  • Stock fell 50% ($98 -> $49). P/E went from ~4x (peak) to 145x/negative (trough).

Cycle 3 (2024-present):

  • Shortage: AI/HBM demand. Revenue hit $23.9B in a single quarter (Q2 FY2026). EPS $12.20. All vendors sold out through 2026.
  • Forward P/E already compressing to 10-12x despite 39% earnings beat. Stock pulled back from $461 to $377 despite record-shattering results.
  • Baker position: Long MU $411M common + $200M put hedge. He believes his own thesis about the inevitable cycle turn.
  • Structural differences this cycle: HBM uses 3x wafer area per bit, only 3 suppliers globally, non-cancellable contracts. But all three vendors expanding capacity simultaneously — the classic setup for the next glut.

The Paradox (all three cycles): Buy MU when P/E is high/negative (trough), sell when P/E is low (peak). This is the opposite of how most investors think about valuation.

2. Foundry — TSM

TSMC’s 2019-2026 trajectory is textbook Baker:

  • 2021 shortage: Utilization 100%+ across all nodes. Lead times 20-30 weeks. Forward P/E compressed from ~30x (early 2021) to ~20x (late 2021) despite 18% revenue growth. Stock went sideways from Feb 2021 peak of $142 through year-end.
  • 2022-2023 glut: Utilization dropped to ~65-80%. Mature node utilization fell below 60%. Capex cut 15% (TSMC almost never cuts capex). Revenue fell ~22% peak-to-trough quarterly.
  • Stock bottomed at $60 in Oct 2022 — 9 months before the revenue trough (Q2 2023). Revenue was still HIGHER at the stock trough than at the stock peak.
  • 2023 recovery: Stock gained 40% while revenue was still declining. The market “looked through” the trough to AI-driven recovery.
  • 2024-2026: Stock rallied to $200-212. 3.5x from trough. N3/N5 utilization back to 100%+.

Key data point: Stock peaked (Feb 2021 at $142) 18 months before revenue peaked (Q3 2022 at $20.2B). Stock troughed (Oct 2022 at $60) 9 months before revenue troughed (Q2 2023 at $15.7B). The stock leads fundamentals in both directions.

Cycle length from peak shortage (mid-2021) to peak glut (mid-2023): exactly 2 years — matching Baker’s framework.

3. Equipment — ASML, LRCX, AMAT

Equipment companies sit at the top of the capex cascade — they see boom orders first and cancellations first.

ASML (the purest example):

  • Bookings surged to EUR 8.9B/quarter (Q3 2022) during peak shortage. P/E hit 88x — highest in a decade.
  • Bookings collapsed to EUR 2.6B (Q3 2024) — a 71% decline. Stock fell 55% even though revenue kept growing (backlog lag of 2+ years).
  • Oct 15, 2024: Bookings missed consensus by 53%. Stock fell 15.7% in one session — worst day since IPO.
  • The market punished ASML not for bad current earnings, but for the implication that the order boom was fading.

LRCX (memory-exposed):

  • 47% of revenue from NAND at peak. When memory customers cut capex ~40%, NAND spending collapsed >75%.
  • Stock fell 56.8% — steepest of the three equipment names.
  • Inverted P/E pattern: P/E was lowest (~20-25x) during the revenue boom and highest (~37x) at the revenue trough. Classic cyclical stock behavior — the market pays a premium on trough earnings.

AMAT (dampened cycle):

  • Revenue never actually declined — growth decelerated from +34% to +3%. China front-loading before export controls masked organic downturn.
  • Stock still fell 49% despite steady earnings. P/E compressed to 18.6x (below 10-year average).

Meta-pattern across all three: Stock peaked 6-12 months before revenue peaked, and troughed 6-12 months before revenue troughed. Watch bookings, not revenue.

4. Auto/Analog — TXN, ON, NXPI, ADI (The Clearest Proof)

The 2021 auto chip shortage is the most visible shortage/glut cycle in recent history and the strongest evidence for Baker’s thesis.

The shortage (2020-2022):

  • Auto OEMs canceled chip orders during COVID (Q2 2020), then scrambled to re-order (Q4 2020). Foundries had already reallocated capacity.
  • Compounding shocks: Texas deep freeze, Renesas fire, Malaysian COVID outbreaks.
  • Lead times stretched from ~12 weeks (normal) to 27+ weeks (May 2022 peak). Some auto MCUs hit 38-52+ weeks.
  • Double/triple ordering amplified the bullwhip effect. Revenue surged: TXN +27% (2021), ON +28%, NXPI +28%.

The glut (2023-2024):

  • Customers consumed 6-12 months of safety stock, stopped ordering. Mobileye discovered customers had accumulated 6-7 million excess EyeQ chips.
  • Lead times collapsed from 27+ weeks to <15 weeks by Q4 2023. Some analog parts went below 8 weeks — shorter than pre-pandemic norms.
  • Revenue declines: TXN -22% ($20.0B -> $15.6B), ON -28% ($8.3B -> $6.0B), ADI -23%.

The P/E data — Baker’s thesis in a single data point:

TXN Peak Earnings (Q3 2022) Trough Earnings (Q3 2024)
Revenue $20.0B/yr $15.6B/yr
EPS $9.65 $5.42
Stock Price $154.78 $206.57
P/E 16.0x (decade low) 38.1x

TXN’s stock was $52 higher during the glut ($207) than at peak earnings ($155). The P/E more than doubled from 16x to 38x. The market assigned the lowest multiple when earnings were highest, and the highest multiple when earnings were lowest. This is the Peter Lynch / Gavin Baker pattern: buy cyclicals at high P/E (depressed earnings), sell at low P/E (peak earnings).

ON Semiconductor was even more extreme: P/E went from 14-17x at peak earnings to 187x at the trough (EPS collapsed to $0.29/quarter).

5. GPU — NVDA, AMD

The GPU market experienced the fastest shortage-to-glut-to-shortage transition in semiconductor history.

Gaming/Crypto shortage (2020-2021):

  • RTX 3080 launched at $699, sold for $1,400-$2,000+ (200%+ MSRP). 25% of GPU shipments went to crypto miners.
  • NVDA gaming revenue peaked at $12.46B (FY2022). Stock ran from ~$6 to ~$33 (split-adjusted), a 5.5x move.

Crypto crash glut (2022):

  • Ethereum Merge (Sep 15, 2022): GPU mining became obsolete overnight. Millions of mining GPUs flooded the market.
  • NVDA gaming revenue crashed 54% quarterly ($3.42B -> $1.57B). Took ~$2B+ in inventory write-downs.
  • Stock fell 66% to $11.22 (split-adjusted) in Oct 2022. Trailing P/E hit ~175x (collapsed earnings).
  • Glut duration: ~12 months from onset to inventory normalization.

AI shortage (2023-present):

  • ChatGPT launched Nov 30, 2022 — while NVIDIA was still clearing inventory. H100 lead times exceeded 50+ weeks by mid-2023.
  • Data center revenue: $3.6B/quarter (Jan 2023) -> $62.3B/quarter (Jan 2026). 17x increase in 3 years.
  • Stock went from $11 to $148+ (13x). But earnings grew ~10x, so the forward P/E actually compressed from 60x+ to 21x. Baker’s thesis confirmed: the market refuses to capitalize shortage-level earnings at peak multiples.

Baker’s position: $1.01B in NVDA ($653M calls + $359M common) — his highest-conviction bet. $2.2B QQQ puts as cycle hedge. Exited AMD entirely (winner-take-most conviction). He acknowledges the “iron law” applies to AI compute but believes demand growth will outpace supply additions for longer than expected.

6. Broadcom — AVGO (The Counter-Example)

AVGO largely avoided the classic shortage/glut cycle through two mechanisms:

  1. Hock Tan’s supply discipline: Deliberately under-shipped during the 2021 shortage, preventing downstream inventory buildup. When peers saw orders collapse in 2022-2023, Broadcom’s drawdown was minimal.
  2. Software revenue ballast: 42% of revenue from subscription software (VMware, CA, Symantec) doesn’t cycle with semiconductor demand.

Revenue never actually declined in any year. The 2022 correction (-36% stock decline) was driven by Fed rate hikes and VMware deal uncertainty, not a business downturn. P/E compressed to 19.4x (5-year low) then re-expanded to 67x as the market repriced AVGO as a secular AI + software compounder.

Baker does not hold AVGO. It’s already priced as a secular compounder, not a cyclical mispricing. Baker prefers to deploy capital into pure-play bottleneck names at compressed multiples (COHR, ALAB, MU).

Lesson: Companies can partially escape the cycle through supply discipline and revenue diversification — but even AVGO’s stock gets hit by sector-wide sentiment during downturns.

The Iron Laws — Synthesized

From Baker’s public commentary (Nov 2023 tweet thread, Dec 2025 “Invest Like the Best” EP.451) and the evidence above:

Law 1: Every shortage is followed by a glut

Confirmed across all 6 subsectors. The cycle length from peak shortage to peak glut ranges from 12-24 months, consistent with Baker’s 2-3 year framework (peak-to-trough-to-new-peak is 3-5 years).

Auto/Analog May 2022 (lead times) Q4 2023 (lead times) ~18 months
GPU (gaming/crypto) Late 2021 Mid-2022 ~6-9 months

Law 2: Multiple compression during shortage

Confirmed across all subsectors — the most counterintuitive finding:

Ticker P/E at Peak Earnings P/E at Trough Earnings Multiple Expansion at Trough
MU 2.96x (FY2018) 145x / negative (FY2023) 49x+
TSM 20-22x (2021) 12-13x (Oct 2022) then 16-18x (2023) Compressed at both extremes

TXN 16x (Q3 2022) 38x (Q3 2024) 2.4x expansion

The meta-lesson: For cyclical semiconductors, a low P/E is a sell signal and a high P/E is a buy signal. The market correctly anticipates the cycle turn and refuses to capitalize peak earnings at growth multiples.

Law 3: Stocks lead fundamentals by 6-12 months

Consistent across every subsector:

  • TSM: Stock peaked Feb 2021, revenue peaked Q3 2022 (18-month lead). Stock troughed Oct 2022, revenue troughed Q2 2023 (9-month lead). .
  • TXN: Stock peaked Nov 2021, revenue peaked Q3 2022 (10-month lead). Stock essentially troughed in late 2023, revenue troughed Q1 2024 (3-6 month lead).
  • ASML: Stock peaked Nov 2021, bookings peaked Q3 2022 (10-month lead).

Law 4: Lead times are the signal

Baker’s actionable indicator: rising lead times = sell the rally; falling lead times = buy the dip.

Counter-Arguments — Why “This Time Is Different” (Maybe)

The strongest counter-arguments to Baker’s framework being applied to the current AI cycle:

  1. Concentrated supply: Leading-edge logic (TSMC ~90%), HBM (3 vendors), EUV (ASML monopoly). Fewer players = more supply discipline = less violent gluts than when 15+ DRAM makers competed in the 1990s.

  2. Capital intensity: A leading-edge fab costs $20-40B and takes 3-5 years. Supply additions are slower and more deliberate than in prior cycles, potentially extending shortage phases.

  3. Demand diversity: AI compute demand comes from training (lumpy) AND inference (steady-state, growing). Even if training plateaus, inference may sustain utilization. Baker himself acknowledges: “every time you use AI takes compute in a way that traditional software just did not.”

  4. Non-cancellable contracts: HBM and CoWoS capacity sold under long-term agreements. This reduces but doesn’t eliminate the inventory cycle.

  5. Geopolitical constraints: Export controls, CHIPS Act subsidies, and reshoring create artificial supply constraints that wouldn’t exist in a purely market-driven cycle.

Baker’s response: He doesn’t dispute these structural differences. He hedges with puts ($2.2B QQQ) rather than exiting the trade. His framework says the direction of the cycle is predictable even if the timing is not. The current cycle may be longer and larger, but the pattern will repeat.

Notable skeptics and allies:

  • Stanley Druckenmiller: Reduced NVDA in 2024, citing valuation and cycle concerns
  • Dylan Patel (SemiAnalysis): Tends bullish on duration of AI buildout but acknowledges inventory cycle risk
  • WSTS data: Global semi industry has shown consistent 3-5 year peak-to-peak cycles for 40+ years (downturns: 1985, 1990-91, 1996-97, 2001, 2008-09, 2012, 2015-16, 2019, 2022-23)

Baker’s Playbook — How He Trades It

Synthesized from his public commentary, 13F filings, and podcast appearances:

  1. Buy when: Industry is in a downturn, inventories elevated, stocks de-rated, sentiment terrible, capex being cut. High P/E on depressed earnings.
  2. Sell/hedge when: Lead times extend, capex guidance rises, euphoric sentiment, “this time is different” narratives. Low P/E on peak earnings.
  3. Use options, not binary bets: Long common + put hedge (e.g., MU $411M long + $200M puts). Calls for leverage when conviction is high (NVDA $653M in calls). The cycle is predictable in direction but not timing — puts protect against being early.
  4. Concentrate on the dominant player during shortage: In a supply-constrained market, the #1 player captures disproportionate value. Hence NVDA over AMD (exited), and COHR over smaller optical names.
  5. Macro hedge the portfolio: $2.2B QQQ puts protect the entire AI infra portfolio against a broad market downturn or cycle turn, without requiring precise timing.

Baker’s framework predicts multiple compression is already underway (MU forward P/E compressing from 20x+ to 10-12x) and a glut phase in 2027-2028 if supply additions overshoot demand.
