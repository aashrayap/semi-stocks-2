# NVIDIA's Margin Illusion

**Author:** Justin Wang (@jstwng)
**Platform:** X (Article)
**Date:** 2026-04-12
**URL:** https://x.com/jstwng (article post)

---

An NVIDIA H100 GPU costs approximately $6,400 to produce and sells for $30,000–$40,000, resulting in a staggering implied chip-level gross margin of roughly 82% [1]. The market has interpreted this as confirmation of the chip designer's durable pricing power, but the truth is that NVIDIA's margin structure is the most exposed in the AI hardware stack and can be better understood as a residual — the surplus left over after paying two irreplaceable supply-constrained monopolies, and before selling to a customer base that is actively trying to replace it.

NVIDIA captures more than 75% of the GPU's selling price while only contributing approximately 14% to its Bill of Materials (BoM), and the gap between the two is its biggest vulnerability. NVIDIA's contribution to the physical product encompasses RTL design, verification and test IP, GDSII mask tapeout, and the CUDA/NVLink software ecosystem that makes its resulting silicon programmable. The other 86% flows to suppliers NVIDIA depends on but does not control: high-bandwidth memory (HBM), supplied by a three-player oligopoly (SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron), accounts for $2,900 per GPU, or 45% of total production cost; advanced packaging and yield losses together exceed the cost of the logic die itself; TSMC captures the remainder through fabrication and CoWoS packaging; and substrate and interposer suppliers take the rest [1].

What makes NVIDIA's gross margin structurally different from, and especially vulnerable compared to, a typical hardware company's is that both ends of its business are controlled by concentrated counterparties with independent pricing power. Its suppliers are monopolists and oligopolists who currently charge below their theoretical maximum, still semi-anchored to pricing norms from a pre-AI GPU market. And its customers form an oligopsony of four hyperscalers who currently pay above their long-run willingness, sustained by a CapEx race in which no individual player can afford to be the first to pull back. NVIDIA's margin sits between rising supply costs and contracting AI accelerator prices, and the residual is compressing accordingly.

## The Bilateral Monopoly NVIDIA Can't Escape

The TSMC-NVIDIA relationship is a textbook bilateral monopoly. NVIDIA cannot source leading-edge fabrication or CoWoS packaging elsewhere; no other foundry can produce Blackwell or Rubin at scale, since: Samsung Foundry's 3nm and 2nm yields remain in the 30–60% range (against TSMC's 70%+ at comparable nodes) and delayed its 1.4nm node by approximately two years to stabilize production; and Intel Foundry Services' 18A node uses incompatible design rules that would require a prohibitively expensive re-architecture of Blackwell's 208 billion transistors [26]. On the other hand, TSMC cannot easily replace NVIDIA's demand since it consumes approximately 60% of global CoWoS capacity (translating to roughly 595,000 wafers in 2026).

Bilateral monopoly theory predicts that pricing outcomes depend on relative bargaining power and mutual benefit maximization rather than competitive market clearing. The critical observation is that TSMC currently captures roughly 3–4% of GPU selling price [2]. For a chip that retails at $30,000+, TSMC's fabrication and packaging revenue per unit is measured in hundreds of dollars for the logic wafer and additional hundreds for CoWoS — far below any reasonable theoretical maximum. TSMC's current pricing reflects a historical equilibrium set when GPUs were $1,000 gaming products rather than $40,000 AI accelerators. The rational equilibrium that follows involves TSMC capturing progressively more of the value it creates, constrained only at the point at which NVIDIA's incremental return on chip design investment falls below its cost of capital, i.e., the point at which the chip designer's incremental ROIC on R&D no longer exceeds its WACC of approximately 11%.

TSMC is already moving. The fab announced 5–10% price hikes across all its sub-5nm nodes starting January 2026, with cumulative increases on 3nm-family nodes expected to reach double digits over the next several years [3]. Its gross margins are guided at 63–65% for early 2026, up from 62.3% in Q4 2025, with long-term targets of "56% and higher" even after the drag from overseas fabs [4]. The fab pure-play currently captures roughly $877–922 per H100 [2], which amounts to just 3–4% of the GPU's $25,000–30,000 selling price. As follows, the arithmetic of chip production-level repricing shows us that a 30% across-the-board TSMC price increase, far beyond anything the company has signaled, would add only $260–280 in marginal cost per unit, less than 1% of GPU-level revenue and easily absorbed within NVIDIA's current margin buffer. We can reasonably predict that TSMC's repricing trajectory, anchored by CoWoS packaging lock-in and revenue, has structural room to run for years before approaching the theoretical ceiling implied by the value of its fabrication and packaging services.

The packaging layer compounds the leverage. It is CoWoS capacity, rather than wafer starts, chip design, or memory supply, that is the binding constraint on AI chip production. CoWoS capacity reached approximately 32,000 wafers per month at end-2024, has scaled to roughly 75,000 as of early 2026, and targets 130,000 by late 2026 [5]. Despite nearly quadrupling in two years, demand grows at 113% annually and capacity remains fully booked through 2026 [6]. Every Blackwell GPU and every Rubin architecture chip requires CoWoS-L packaging because these accelerators use 2.5D interposer-based integration to connect the logic die with multiple HBM stacks at the memory bandwidth densities (8+ TB/s) that training and inference workloads demand; no conventional flip-chip or fan-out packaging achieves the necessary inter-die bandwidth [3]. Further, when a customer commits to TSMC for both front-end fabrication and back-end advanced packaging, switching costs compound multiplicatively, as the customer is locked in at two stages of manufacturing simultaneously [3]. No other company can replicate CoWoS-L at the scale AI accelerators require. And, while OSAT vendors grew 10% in 2025, they cannot match TSMC's structural packaging monopoly. The Taiwanese chip fab's pricing power remains notably under-exercised.

## A Memory Squeeze

HBM totals 45% of GPU production cost, and the share rises with each new node because the underlying demand drivers compound rather than cycle. Three forces are responsible: KV cache scaling, as context windows extend from tens of thousands of tokens to over a million (GPT-5.3 supports 400K tokens; Claude 4.6 and Gemini 3.0 Pro both operate at 1M+); the shift from dense transformer architectures to sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, where all expert sub-networks must remain resident in memory even though only a fraction activate per token (Mixtral 8x22B loads 176 billion parameters to activate 39 billion per forward pass); and rack-level memory co-scaling enabled by interconnect bandwidth doubling each generation, from 900 GB/s (NVLink 4, H100) to 1.8 TB/s (NVLink 5, B200) to a projected 3.6 TB/s (NVLink 6), with the eventual transition to co-packaged optics de-bottlenecking the link between memory and compute [8]. Per-GPU memory specifications showcase the trend: 80 GB HBM3 for the H100, 141 GB HBM3e for the H200, 192 GB HBM3e for the B200 (2.4x in a single generation), 288 GB HBM3e for the B300, and a projected 288 GB HBM4 for Vera Rubin — a 3.6x increase across three architectural generations [8]. Per-rack memory scales even faster as GPU count per system increases: GB200 NVL72 racks aggregate approximately 10.2 TB total HBM, GB300 NVL72 40 TB, and Vera Rubin NVL144 41.5 TB [8].

Each step up in memory content translates directly into a larger share of GPU BoM flowing to the memory oligopoly rather than to NVIDIA. The memory vendors know this and are pricing accordingly. Samsung and SK Hynix have negotiated approximately 20% price increases on HBM3E contracts for 2026 [9]. DRAM spot prices nearly tripled year-over-year by Q4 2025 [10]. SK Hynix posted CY2025 gross margins of approximately 68.8%, and Micron's Q2 FY2026 GAAP gross margin reached 74.9% [11]. For the first time in semiconductor history, memory company gross margins are approaching and in some quarters exceeding TSMC's [12]. The three-player oligopoly controls 95%+ of global DRAM production, capacity is fully sold out through 2027, and long-term contracts are dampening the spot-market volatility that historically enabled buyers to play suppliers against each other [10]. It is of note that NVIDIA has $53 billion in memory purchase commitments — more than $25 billion of which extends beyond next quarter — which points toward a company securing allocation on the oligopoly's terms because the alternative (insufficient HBM supply constraining GPU shipments) would be far more destructive to revenue than any margin concession on input costs.

NVIDIA's margin squeeze comes from two directions simultaneously, as TSMC raises fabrication and packaging prices 5–10% annually from below, and memory vendors raise HBM prices 20%+ from the side. NVIDIA's margin absorbs both, because it is the residual between what it pays upstream and what it charges downstream. As such, its GAAP gross margin has already compressed from 75.0% in FY2025 to 71.1% for the full fiscal year FY2026, with R&D intensity at 8.6% of revenue and operating margin at 62% [14] (management guided for "mid-70s" going forward). While markets are pricing mid-70s as a floor, the hardware stack's consecutive bilateral monopolies point toward a structural ceiling.

## The Customer Base Is a Ticking Clock

NVIDIA's revenue concentration compounds the vulnerability from yet another direction. Hyperscalers Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta represent more than 40% of NVIDIA's revenue and are simultaneously investing billions into their own custom silicon alternatives [15]. Google's TPU v7 (Ironwood) delivers 4,614 peak FP8 TFLOPS with 192 GB HBM and 7.37 TB/s memory bandwidth, matching NVIDIA's B200 on compute density (4,500 FP8 TFLOPS) while exceeding it on memory bandwidth [16][27]. Amazon's Trainium2 delivers competitive training throughput at roughly one-third the per-chip hourly cost ($1/hr vs. $3/hr for H100), and Trainium3 projects 4x the compute performance of its predecessor [17][18][27]. Microsoft's Maia 200 is deploying in Azure data centers [14]. And AMD's MI300X offers 2,615 FP8 TFLOPS with 192 GB HBM at a list price of approximately $15,000, against the H100's $25,000–$30,000 for 80 GB and 1,979 FP8 TFLOPS. Broadcom, a key design partner to these hyperscalers, has a $73 billion order backlog, of which roughly $53 billion is comprised of custom AI accelerators [15].

NVIDIA's 75% gross margin is their cost of compute procurement. Cloud providers operating at gross margins of 50–68% on compute services (AWS at roughly 35% operating margin, Azure's cloud gross margin declining from 70% to 68% as AI infrastructure costs rise [25]) are paying a 75%-margin tax to their primary silicon supplier, a margin that exceeds every other major semiconductor company in the AI stack: TSMC at 58–65%, Broadcom's semiconductor segment at approximately 68% (AVGO's blended 77.5% figure is inflated by VMware's 93% software gross margins), AMD at roughly 44%, and the memory vendors themselves at anywhere from 69–75% [14][24][11][12]. Custom silicon programs are the rational response to a supplier extracting more economic rent than its customers can sustainably absorb.

This is all a long-winded way of pointing out that the AI hardware stack's largest structural misalignment is NVIDIA's GAAP gross margin exceeding that of every supplier whose components constitute 86% of the GPU's BoM, despite only contributing 14% of the physical product.

A corollary: custom ASICs do not need to succeed at scale to compress NVIDIA's margin. Rather, they need only to exist as a credible alternative that gives hyperscalers bargaining leverage. The actual track record of custom silicon outside Google is mixed — Amazon swapped ASIC design partners between Trainium2 and Trainium3 (Marvell to Alchip), Microsoft's Maia is late and narrowly scoped to inference, and Meta's MTIA is nascent [17][14]. The custom ASIC TAM projections (a third of compute by 2030, 44% CAGR [19]) likely overshoot because they assume architectural stability that rapid shifts in model design (transition from dense models to MoE, test-time compute scaling) actively undermine.

The math on displacement clarifies the second-order effect. NVIDIA's data center revenue in FY2026 was approximately $115 billion [14]. If custom ASICs capture 15% of the AI accelerator market by 2028, a conservative estimate relative to the bulls' 33–45% projections, and if that share comes disproportionately from NVIDIA's hyperscaler customers, NVIDIA faces roughly $17–20 billion in displaced revenue at current pricing. The revenue loss matters noticeably less than the second-order effect itself: NVIDIA cannot maintain 75% gross margins when its four largest customers can credibly redirect 15–25% of incremental accelerator spend to in-house alternatives that, whatever their absolute performance limitations, can cost up to 30–40% less per inference FLOP on targeted workloads [18]. SemiAnalysis's InferenceX benchmarks corroborate the price-performance convergence, showing that AMD's MI355X delivers cost-per-token materially below NVIDIA's GB300 NVL72 at high concurrency on DeepSeek R1 reasoning workloads, and that Trainium2 achieves 50–70% lower cost per billion tokens versus H100 clusters for training [28].

But the market-share outcome is irrelevant to the margin argument. What matters is the bargaining dynamic, and the formal mechanism is well-established: Nash (1950) and Rubinstein (1982) showed that in any negotiation, the party with the superior BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement) captures a disproportionate share of the cooperative surplus. Three years ago, hyperscalers had no credible BATNA, as custom silicon programs were nascent, unproven, and architecturally narrow. Today, Google's TPU v7 matches Blackwell on FP8 compute density at 4,614 TFLOPS per chip with 192 GB of HBM [27]; Amazon's Trainium2 offers competitive training throughput at a third of the per-chip cost; and AMD's MI325X delivers 2,615 FP8 TFLOPS with 256 GB HBM3e [27]. These hyperscalers represent a substantial share of NVIDIA's data center revenue and can credibly threaten to shift incremental spend to in-house silicon. NVIDIA cannot price as if it faces zero competition when its largest customers have viable alternatives in production. The threat disciplines pricing even if total ASIC market share plateaus at 20–25% rather than reaching the 33–45% that custom silicon bulls are projecting. Economic theory contends that asymmetry in outside options compresses the seller's share of the surplus, which, in NVIDIA's income statement, should manifest as gross margin compression.

The CapEx-to-revenue gap amplifies the pressure. Microsoft targeted $25 billion in AI-related revenue for fiscal 2026 against $97.7–150 billion in capital expenditure [21]. AI-related services across all hyperscalers were expected to deliver only about $25 billion in revenue in 2025, roughly 10% of what those same companies spent on infrastructure [22]. Only about 25% of AI initiatives have delivered their expected ROI to date [22], and Larry Page stated he was "willing to go bankrupt rather than lose this race" [23]. Industry-wide data center CapEx numbers from 2023 onward have proven that hyperscalers have justified spending because the potential cost of not doing so outweighs the potential losses incurred by unprofitable buildouts. When the spending eventually rationalizes, whether through demonstrated ROI or capital markets discipline, the first cost to be renegotiated will be the hardware supplier commanding the highest margin. And the hyperscalers will walk into that negotiation with in-house silicon programs that, whatever their limitations, provide a credible BATNA that did not exist three years ago.

## Where is the Surplus Actually Accruing?

We can interpret the AI hardware value chain as a nested series of bilateral oligopolies, where, at each node, the entity with scarcer capacity and fewer substitutes captures a disproportionate share of the surplus. The market prices the stack as if NVIDIA occupies the scarcest node, but substitutability, supply constraint duration, and pricing-power trajectory all point elsewhere.

Ranking the layers by substitutability and supply constraint:

**First** (most supply-constrained and least substitutable): TSMC's advanced packaging platform, CoWoS. Zero alternatives exist at scale, and demand compounds at 113% CAGR against capacity that requires 2+ years of investment to expand, with each new packaging generation (CoWoS-L for Blackwell, CoWoS-next for Rubin) demanding retooling that extends lead times further. Every AI accelerator produced globally, whether designed by NVIDIA, Google, Amazon, AMD, or any Broadcom ASIC customer, routes through TSMC advanced packaging. The fab's pricing power is structural and has not been exercised to anywhere near its theoretical maximum: TSMC, a business with 92% share of sub-5nm logic and a packaging monopoly facing 113% demand CAGR, is trading at approximately 25x forward P/E, a discount to the S&P 500 Information Technology's broader sector average of roughly 32x [4][24].

**Second:** HBM memory. Three firms, SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron, control 95%+ of global production, capacity is fully committed through 2027, and revenue per wafer runs at 3–5x that of conventional DRAM. The industry's transition away from spot-market pricing toward long-term supply agreements has compressed the price volatility that historically defined memory as a cyclical trade. Memory company gross margins now approach and in some quarters exceed TSMC's, yet the market continues to price them at 5–8x forward earnings [24].

**Third:** Leading-edge logic fabrication, TSMC's wafer-start business on N3, N3E, N2, and successor nodes. TSMC holds 92% market share in sub-5nm, but the constraint here is less acute than in packaging or memory because wafer capacity is expandable on 18–24 month timelines, pricing is relatively mature after decades of Moore's Law scaling economics, and the customer base for leading-edge logic extends well beyond AI. TSMC's 5–10% annual price increases on sub-5nm compound meaningfully, but the capacity bottleneck is less binding [3][4].

**Fourth** (least supply-constrained and most substitutable): GPU/accelerator design. At this layer, NVIDIA is dominant in training, possessing a genuine software ecosystem advantage in CUDA, and generating $115 billion in data center revenue in FY2026 [14], but selling to a customer base where the four largest buyers collectively fund in-house alternatives that provide credible bargaining leverage in every procurement negotiation, regardless of whether custom silicon captures the market share that bulls project. The CUDA moat is real but faces erosion from Triton, ROCm, and SYCL, and the competitive pressure that matters for NVIDIA's margin is ASICs existing as a credible outside option that disciplines pricing rather than ASICs replacing GPUs en masse.

The market has assigned multiples in roughly the inverse order: NVIDIA at 37x trailing earnings and 46x EV/FCF; TSMC at approximately 25x forward P/E; and memory companies at 5–12x forward P/E [14][24]. The most competitively exposed layer currently commands the highest multiple, while the most structurally entrenched layers are trading at the deepest discounts.

## So What?

NVDA's inflated gross margin is exposed to the gap between the market's pricing-power assumptions and where the AI hardware value chain's actual bargaining-power distribution exists. This is not to say that NVIDIA's revenue will decline or that AI demand is overstated; I believe that the chip designer's revenue will likely continue growing as the CapEx cycle extends through 2027–2028. My argument is narrower and more structurally focused — that because NVIDIA's gross margin is the variable that absorbs pressure from every other layer of the stack, the market has not priced future margin compression accordingly. Even the assumptions embedded in street-consensus DCF models (gross margin declining from 74% to 71% over a five-year projection period, per standard sell-side figures) implicitly concede the directional thesis while understating the magnitude of simultaneous, bilateral pressure from suppliers and customers [14].

The larger directional call is that the market is overweight the chip design layer and underweight the physical infrastructure layers. TSMC's advanced packaging business remains buried inside the broader foundry and is the most underpriced monopoly in the AI hardware stack; the memory oligopoly has structural durability that has not fully priced in yet; and NVIDIA's margin, the income statement statistic its $4.5 trillion market cap is founded on, is, at its core, a residual that every other participant in the value chain has both the incentive and the leverage to compress.

Compute is no longer an infinitely elastic scale-out expense, and value accrual goes not to "who sells the most GPUs," but rather to "who captures the most surplus per dollar of AI infrastructure deployed." NVIDIA, increasingly, is not the AI winner the market thinks it is.

[^1]: TSMC's per-wafer pricing for N3 and N5 nodes evolved over a decade of incremental negotiation with fabless semiconductor customers whose GPU average selling prices rarely exceeded $1,000. CoWoS interposer packaging adds another $200–$500 per unit at current pricing, a fraction of the value the packaging enables. TSMC could plausibly double its per-wafer revenue from AI accelerators and still leave NVIDIA with margin sufficient to justify continued design investment, but the path from current pricing to that equilibrium will involve years of incremental contract renegotiation rather than a single repricing event.

[^2]: This pattern of under-captured surplus is not unique to the TSMC-NVIDIA node. One layer further down the stack, ASML holds a bilateral monopoly with TSMC over EUV lithography, with zero substitutes for its high-NA EUV systems, yet ASML's pricing has historically left substantial value with TSMC rather than extracting the theoretical maximum. Multiple layers of the AI hardware stack currently price below their bargaining-power ceiling, and the repricing at each layer compounds against the participant whose margin is most fragile and absorbs the cumulative pressure.

[^3]: The parallel to energy commodity markets is instructive. When LNG buyers shifted from spot procurement to 15–20 year take-or-pay contracts in the 2010s, the result was a structural compression of LNG price volatility and a re-rating of major LNG suppliers (Shell, TotalEnergies, Cheniere Energy) from cyclical commodity producers to quasi-utility businesses with more predictable cash flows. Equity valuations adjusted accordingly as the market recognized that long-term contracted revenue reduced the mean-reverting price behavior that had historically justified deep cyclical discounts. HBM contracting is trending in the same direction as 18–24 month forward commitments continue replacing spot-market exposure, negotiated price increases are baked into multi-year agreements, and a three-player supply structure eliminates the buyer's ability to exploit cyclical troughs by playing producers against each other. If the LNG analogy holds, the current 5–8x forward P/E multiples on memory companies embed a cyclical discount that the underlying business structure no longer warrants [10].

[^4]: The compounding mechanism works as follows. When TSMC raises CoWoS packaging prices by 10% and HBM vendors raise memory prices by 20% simultaneously, NVIDIA can either absorb both increases in its gross margin (accepting compression), pass the increases through to customers (risking demand destruction or accelerating the shift to in-house silicon), or some combination of the two. In practice, because the hyperscaler customer base has growing bargaining leverage via custom silicon alternatives, NVIDIA's ability to pass through supplier price increases is constrained. The squeeze thus operates like a vise wherein suppliers tighten from below and customers' outside options limit the pass-through from above. The same dynamic played out in the ASML-TSMC relationship during the EUV transition, when ASML raised prices substantially (from ~$120M per EUV tool in 2017 to $380M+ for high-NA EUV) and TSMC could only partially pass those increases through to fabless customers because Intel and Samsung provided, however imperfect, alternative sourcing options [2][3].

[^5]: The hyperscaler CapEx dynamic has been widely described as a multi-player prisoner's dilemma in which each company's dominant strategy is to continue investing in AI infrastructure regardless of near-term returns, because unilateral restraint cedes market position to rivals who continue spending. Microsoft targeted $25 billion in AI-related revenue for fiscal 2026 against $97.7–150 billion in capital expenditure [21]. Only about 25% of enterprise AI initiatives have delivered their expected ROI to date [22]. The individually rational strategy (spend) produces a collectively suboptimal outcome (aggregate overinvestment relative to near-term demand), but no individual player can deviate without accepting worse competitive positioning. This creates a temporary equilibrium in which hyperscalers pay NVIDIA's asking price not because the price is justified by the return on their AI investments, but because the cost of not having GPUs exceeds the cost of overpaying for them.

[^6]: AMD's single node aggregated serving performance per TCO has caught up to that of Nvidia's. As test-time compute continues to scale, hyperscalers have a structural motivation to buoy AMD's business to negotiate leverage against NVIDIA's near-monopoly pricing power: every dollar that AMD extracts from NVIDIA's gross margins is a dollar that hyperscalers capture, allowing for margin reduction on the most TCO-intensive segment of already ballooning data center CapEx budgets.
